Work continues to restore power across the area. 

While some of you saw your power come back in the last 24 hours, others are still waiting. We know the wait is difficult, but we are making progress.

More than 100 skilled crews from Austin Energy and partnering electric utilities continue to work around the clock to get power back. They are facing challenging restoration conditions in neighborhood after neighborhood, and they are overcoming those obstacles.

Since 5 p.m. yesterday, we restored power to nearly 62,000 more customers, for a total of 267,365 customers restored since the beginning of this historic winter event. We will keep working 24/7 to increase these numbers. 


Restoration Progress

Here's why we're making progress:
  • The weather is better. Warming temperatures, melting ice and dry air make this work move more quickly. Our equipment is no longer affected by freezing temps and our crews can better focus on the damages.
  • We have more help. To address the complex and large-scale outages caused by the ice, we needed to quickly bring on more trained professionals. Other electric utilities have been partnering with us, sharing their lineworkers and their equipment to expedite repairs and get you back online.
  • More time means more progress. So many of these repairs need more people AND more time. They need time for extensive, repetitive and methodical work that requires careful safety measures and expert skill to fully address. Crews have had more time, which means they are making more progress.
  • Less ice means less weight. Without the weight of ice on the stressed branches, fewer trees and branches are falling, breaking and causing new problems with power lines. If you have a downed line in your yard or see parts of a tree on a power line, do not approach and stay at least 35 feet away. Call 512-322-9100 to report. Only call 911 if you see sparks.
Challenges to our progress:

The extensive ice accumulation on electric equipment and tree branches caused electrical distribution lines and nearby vegetation to sag, break or come into contact with one another. This kind of damage is complex to repair, and these complex repairs are required all over the city. 

Restoration crews are working through:
  • Multiple hazards with each outage: A power line might need 10 different fixes to get it back online and might take multiple days to repair.
  • Unfallen trees and branches: Some trees and branches are still weak. After our crews restore power, compromised vegetation sometimes gives way, causing a new outage.
  • Obstacles reaching the issue: Some repairs are in areas that are hard for our crews to reach. Large tree limbs could be in the way of a repair truck, or the ground is saturated, hindering access. Crew members may knock on your door asking to access Austin Energy equipment. They will be clearly identified with badges and Austin Energy vehicles. Please work with them to allow access.
  • Circuits overloading after coming back on: Because many electronics were on before the outage, they are ready to draw power as soon as it comes down the line. This causes the circuits to work too hard when the power comes back on and can overload the distribution equipment. If your power is not on yet, please unplug/turn off appliances, electronics, thermostats, pool pumps, pool heaters and vehicle chargers left on before the outage to prevent this overload.
  • Cars speeding by job sites: This work needs safety precautions in the work being done and in the surrounding conditions. Please remember to slow down and move over for any utility crews working to restore power and remove debris from power lines. 
Our crews are prepared for these challenges, it just takes time for them to do their work safely.
 
Safety Tips
  • Portable generators can be very dangerous to members of your household and the crews working to restore your power. Keep generators outside and at least 20 feet away from a window, door or vent. Get more portable generator safety tips.
  • Never fuel or use a portable generator, charcoal grill, camp stove or other gasoline- or charcoal-burning device inside your home, basement, place of business or garage.
  • Avoid carbon monoxide poisoning! Never run a car or truck inside a garage, even if you leave the garage door open.
  • If using an electric space heater, be sure it has an automatic shut-off switch and non-glowing elements, and make sure to keep away from flammable materials.
  • Never heat your home with a gas oven or burn anything in a stove or a fireplace that is not vented. Do not burn paper in a fireplace.
